Chapter 1 — Introduction

What Is the Bible Code?

The "Bible Code" refers to a family of methods for finding hidden words, names, or messages within the text of scripture by reading letters at regular intervals — skipping every nth character through a long stretch of text.

The most studied form is called Equidistant Letter Sequences (ELS). A researcher picks a skip value — say, every 7th letter — and reads the resulting sequence to see whether it spells a meaningful word. Because biblical texts are very long, a surprising number of such words do appear, which some interpreters regard as intentional encoding by a divine author.

Important disclaimer: Bible Code research is not scientifically validated. The scientific consensus, established by rigorous statistical work from researchers including Brendan McKay, Dror Bar-Natan, and Gil Kalai, is that ELS patterns in the Bible are no more frequent than in any other text of similar length. This program exists for educational exploration, not to endorse Bible Code claims.

Historical Background

The roots of letter-skipping in Hebrew scripture reach back to the Kabbalah and medieval rabbinical scholarship. Rabbi Bachya ben Asher (13th century) noted that every 42nd letter in the Torah spells the divine name. The modern wave of interest was sparked by the mathematician Eliyahu Rips and his colleagues, who in 1994 published a paper in the journal Statistical Science claiming to find meaningful ELS patterns linking the names of famous rabbis to their birth and death dates. This paper, and Michael Drosnin's popular book The Bible Code (1997), brought the phenomenon to worldwide attention.

The Rips et al. paper triggered an intense scientific debate. Replication attempts by independent teams consistently failed to reproduce the results under stricter controls, and the original findings were attributed to flexibility in how the researchers defined their search terms — a form of selection bias.

How ELS Works

The mechanics are straightforward. Given a continuous string of letters (all spaces and punctuation removed), an ELS search picks a starting position p and a skip value k, then reads the characters at positions p, p+k, p+2k, … and so on until a word of the desired length is formed. For a word of length L, there are roughly N × (2 × maxSkip) possible starting position / skip combinations to check, where N is the length of the text.

A negative skip value reads the sequence backwards — finding words spelled in reverse. This is sometimes called a "reverse ELS."

Example: In a text …ABCDEFGHIJ…, skip=3 starting at position 0 gives A, D, G, J — spelling ADGJ. Starting at position 1 gives B, E, H — spelling BEH, and so forth. Every combination of starting position and skip value is checked.

Scientific Status

The scientific community has reached a clear consensus: ELS patterns in the Bible are statistically inevitable in any long text and carry no evidential weight for hidden authorship or prophetic encoding. Three key arguments explain why:

  • Multiple testing: When you check millions of combinations of starting positions, skip values, and search terms, you are virtually guaranteed to find some matches simply by chance.
  • Selection bias: Researchers tend to report hits and ignore the far more numerous misses, giving a misleading impression of frequency.
  • Control texts: When the same ELS methods are applied to Moby Dick, War and Peace, or even random letter strings, equally striking patterns emerge. This was demonstrated conclusively by McKay et al. (1999) in Statistical Science.

None of this makes ELS exploration uninteresting — it is a fascinating window into combinatorics, linguistics, and the history of scriptural interpretation. It should simply be approached as a tool for discovery and reflection, not proof of anything.

Chapter 2 — ELS Research

Doing ELS Research

Even setting aside questions of statistical significance, working through ELS searches systematically can be a rewarding way to engage deeply with scriptural texts. Here is a methodology that helps you get meaningful results and avoid common traps.

Choosing Your Text

The language and source text you choose significantly affects your results. Consider:

  • Hebrew for the Old Testament is the traditional choice for Bible Code research. The 22-letter Hebrew alphabet (no vowels in the normalized text) makes the combinatorics different from alphabets with vowels.
  • Greek for the New Testament — Koine Greek, as used in the Septuagint and New Testament, is the appropriate source for Christian-oriented searches.
  • Latin (Vulgate) is useful for studying how the Bible was interpreted in Western Christianity for over a millennium.
  • Finnish and English are included for comparative study and to demonstrate that ELS patterns are not unique to Hebrew.

Limit your initial searches to a defined book and chapter range. Searching the entire Bible at once with a broad skip range produces enormous numbers of results that are difficult to evaluate.

Choosing Search Terms

The choice of what to search for is the most important — and most subjective — decision in ELS research. Best practices:

  1. Define your terms before searching. Write down the exact words you intend to search for before you open the program. Changing your search term after seeing results invalidates any conclusions you might draw.
  2. Use the normalized form. The program shows you the normalized version of your search term (e.g. JUMALA for Finnish "jumala"). Make sure this is what you intend to search.
  3. Keep terms short enough to be plausible but long enough to be meaningful. A 2-letter term will appear thousands of times. A 10-letter term may appear zero times. 3–6 letters is usually the most productive range.
  4. Consider semantic context. The most compelling ELS finds are those where a word appears in close proximity to the text that discusses that concept — even though ELS skips ignore verse boundaries.

Interpreting Results

When the search completes, you will see a list of results on the right panel. Each result tells you the skip value, the starting position, and the exact letter sequence found. Things to consider when evaluating results:

What you seeWhat it meansSignificance?
Skip = 1 The word appears as a consecutive sequence in the plain text Expected — not an ELS find
Skip = 2–10, many results Short skip range, common word — statistically expected Low
Skip = 20–50, rare word Less common combination Moderate interest only
Term appears near thematically related plain text Proximity of ELS to surface meaning Most interesting case for reflection

Cluster Analysis

The most famous Bible Code claims involve not just one word appearing at an ELS, but several related words appearing in close proximity to each other in the matrix view. This is called a cluster. The program's cluster search mode lets you specify a primary search term plus a list of related terms, and it will find cases where the primary term's ELS occurs within a set distance of the related terms' ELS.

The proximity threshold controls how far apart (in characters of the continuous normalized text) the primary and related terms are allowed to be. A lower threshold finds tighter clusters; a higher threshold finds looser ones. Start with 500–1000 characters and adjust from there.

Statistical Pitfalls to Avoid

  • Don't fish for results. Running 50 different searches and reporting the 3 that look interesting is the Texas Sharpshooter Fallacy.
  • Compare against a control. Run the exact same search on a different text of similar length (e.g. a Finnish novel). If you find equally striking results, this suggests the pattern is due to chance.
  • Note the total number of possible combinations. The larger your skip range and the longer your text, the more combinations are being checked, and the more matches you expect by pure chance.
  • Be suspicious of post-hoc narrative. It is very easy to construct a meaningful-sounding story around any set of words found in proximity.
Chapter 3 — Using the Program

Program Overview

The Bible Code Explorer is divided into three vertical columns: the Control Panel on the left, the Display Area in the centre, and the Results Panel on the right.

The general workflow is: select a language and text range → enter a search term → run the search → browse results in the right panel → click any result to highlight it in the centre display.

Language Selection

The language dropdown in the top-right header controls which Bible translation is searched. The available options are:

  • Latin (Vulgate) — the 4th-century Latin translation by Jerome, the standard Bible of Western Christianity for over 1,000 years.
  • King James English — the 1611 English translation, one of the most influential texts in the English language.
  • Finnish — the Finnish Bible, using the 29-letter Finnish alphabet including Å, Ä, and Ö as distinct letters.

When the Side-by-Side view is active, a second language panel appears automatically: Hebrew for Old Testament books, Greek for New Testament books.

Text Range

The Text Range section in the control panel lets you choose which book and chapter range to search. The book selector is populated dynamically from the loaded Bible data, grouped into Old and New Testament sections. The chapter fields update automatically when you change the book.

Searching a larger chapter range takes longer and produces more results. For exploratory work, start with a single chapter; for comprehensive study, expand to a full book.

Search Parameters

Search Term

Type the word you want to find. As you type, the Normalized preview shows exactly what will be searched — spaces and punctuation are removed, the text is uppercased, and language-specific rules are applied (e.g. Finnish Ä and Ö are preserved; Latin diacritics are stripped).

Skip Interval Range

Sets the minimum and maximum skip values. Skip 1 finds the word in plain text (not really ELS). Larger values find sparser patterns. The default range of 1 to 50 is a good starting point. For longer texts, consider extending to 100 or 200. Including backward searches doubles the search space by also checking negative skip values.

Max Results

Limits how many results are returned to prevent the browser from becoming slow. The default of 500 is usually sufficient. If you are searching a very common word at a wide skip range, you may hit this limit — in that case, narrow your skip range or increase the limit cautiously.

Text View

The Text View tab shows the continuous normalized text with verse numbers preserved as superscripts. After clicking View in Text on any result, the matching letters are highlighted in amber and the view scrolls to bring the first letter into focus.

Individual letters are clickable — hovering over a letter shows its position in the continuous text, which is useful for understanding the skip arithmetic.

Matrix View

The Matrix View wraps the continuous text into a 2D grid of a chosen width. When an ELS result has a skip value of exactly k, and the matrix width is also k, the matching letters fall in a perfect vertical column — the classic "Bible Code" presentation.

Use Auto-optimize to let the program calculate the matrix width that makes the current result set appear most compact (letters closest together in the grid). You can also type a width manually.

Side-by-Side View

When Show reference language is ticked in the control panel, the Side-by-Side tab shows the primary language alongside Hebrew (for Old Testament) or Greek (for New Testament), aligned verse by verse. Hovering over a word in one pane highlights the corresponding word(s) in the other pane.

This view is particularly useful for comparing how a search term relates to the underlying source language — for instance, seeing how a Finnish word you searched aligns with the original Hebrew text of Genesis.

Results Panel

Every search result is listed on the right with its term, skip value, starting position in the continuous text, the exact letter sequence found, and a View in Text button. Results can be sorted by position or by skip value using the dropdown at the top of the panel.

The status bar at the bottom of the page always shows how many results were found.

Exporting Results

After a successful search, three export options are available:

CSV
A spreadsheet-compatible file with columns for term, skip, start position, end position, sequence, and letter positions. Open in Excel, LibreOffice, or any CSV viewer.
JSON
A complete structured data file including metadata, all search parameters, and all results. Useful for programmatic analysis or archiving.
HTML Matrix
A standalone HTML file showing the matrix view with highlighted cells. Can be shared or printed without needing the application.

Advanced Options

Clicking ⚙️ Advanced Options reveals:

  • Max Results — cap on how many matches are returned.
  • Cluster search mode — enables multi-term proximity searching. Enter related terms separated by commas in the box that appears, and set a proximity threshold in characters.
Chapter 4 — Gematria

What Is Gematria?

Gematria is the practice of assigning numeric values to letters, then using those values to find hidden connections between words, phrases, or concepts that share the same total. It is one of the oldest forms of numerological interpretation in religious and philosophical traditions.

The core idea is simple: if two words calculate to the same number, they are considered to share a deeper equivalence or connection. A scholar might note that the Hebrew word for "peace" (שלום, shalom) has the same gematria value as a particular divine name, and interpret this as a meaningful relationship encoded in the language itself.

Traditions and Systems

Virtually every ancient language tradition with an alphabetic script developed some form of gematria. The practice appears in Hebrew Kabbalistic texts, Greek mystery traditions, Latin medieval scholarship, and has found modern expressions in English and other European languages. The systems differ significantly in their letter values, but the interpretive method is consistent.

Hebrew Systems

Hebrew gematria has the richest tradition and the most developed taxonomy of systems. The program implements three:

SystemHebrew nameHow it worksExample: אמת (Truth)
Mispar Hechrachi מספר הכרחי Absolute values: א=1…י=10, כ=20…ק=100, ר=200, ש=300, ת=400. Final forms equal base form. א(1) + מ(40) + ת(400) = 441
Mispar Gadol מספר גדול Like Hechrachi but final letter forms continue the sequence: ך=500, ם=600, ן=700, ף=800, ץ=900. Same as above unless final forms appear
Mispar Katan מספר קטן Reduced: each letter's place value is collapsed to a single digit. Yod(10)→1, Kaf(20)→2, etc. א(1) + מ(4) + ת(4) = 9

In Mispar Hechrachi, the Hebrew alphabet is divided into three groups of nine: units (א–ט = 1–9), tens (י–צ = 10–90), and hundreds (ק–ת = 100–400). This structure reflects the classical understanding of numbers in ancient Hebrew culture.

Greek Isopsephy

The Greek equivalent of gematria is called isopsephy (ἰσόψηφος — equal pebbles, a reference to counting with stones). The Greek alphabet originally had 27 letters, including three archaic letters (digamma=6, koppa=90, sampi=900) that dropped from everyday use but were retained for numeric purposes. This is why the Greek numeric alphabet has gaps: there is no letter with value 6, 90, or 900 in modern usage.

The most famous example of Greek isopsephy in scripture is the Number of the Beast in Revelation 13:18. The number 666 encodes a name; most modern scholars believe this is Nero Caesar transliterated into Greek.

ΝΕΡΩΝ ΚΑΙΣΑΡ = 666

Latin Systems

Latin gematria exists in two forms in this program:

  • Literae Notae (Roman numeral letters): Only the letters I/J (=1), V/U (=5), X (=10), L (=50), C (=100), D (=500), and M (=1000) have values. All other letters are 0. This is the historically attested system used in medieval Latin textual interpretation.
  • Ordinal (A=1): A simple sequential assignment A=1 through Z=26, identical to the English standard system. This is more practical for computational work and is the default for Latin in this program.

English Systems

English gematria is a modern development, primarily from the 19th and 20th centuries. Three variants are provided:

  • Standard (A=1…Z=26): The most widely used. The word GOD = 7+15+4 = 26.
  • Reduction: Each letter maps to 1–9 by wrapping: A–I = 1–9, J–R = 1–9, S–Z = 1–8. Equivalent to summing the digits of the standard value.
  • Reverse: Z=1, Y=2 … A=26. Used in some modern occult traditions as a "mirror" system.

Finnish System

Finnish gematria follows the same ordinal logic as English but accounts for the three extra letters in the Finnish alphabet. The full order is A through Z (1–26), then Å=27, Ä=28, Ö=29. This means a Finnish word containing Ä or Ö will naturally have a higher gematria value than an equivalent English word of the same apparent structure.

A reduction variant is also provided, applying the same modulo-9 logic to all 29 letters of the Finnish alphabet.

Chapter 5 — Gematria Research

Doing Gematria Research

Gematria research involves calculating values, identifying equalities, and constructing interpretive frameworks around them. Like ELS, it is more art than science — but a disciplined methodology makes the results more interesting and defensible.

Methodology

  1. Choose a system appropriate to your text. Use Mispar Hechrachi for Hebrew scripture, Isopsephy for Greek New Testament, Latin Ordinal for the Vulgate. Mixing systems across languages is valid as a comparative exercise, but note clearly which system you used.
  2. Establish your target value first. Decide what value you are interested in before searching for matches. Starting from a word and looking for other words that match it is more rigorous than starting from a match and working backwards.
  3. Check the digital root. The digital root (repeatedly summing digits until you reach a single digit) can reveal structural similarities between numbers that look different at first. The program shows digital roots automatically.
  4. Document your calculations transparently. Always show the per-letter breakdown so others can verify your arithmetic.
  5. Compare across systems. A particularly meaningful equivalence might hold in one system but not another. Noting this strengthens or weakens the interpretive claim.

Famous Historical Examples

The name of God in the Tetragrammaton יהוה (YHWH) = 10+5+6+5 = 26 in Mispar Hechrachi. The English word GOD also equals 26 in Standard English gematria. This is one of the most cited cross-language gematria equivalences, though its significance is disputed since the systems were developed independently.

Cross-Language Comparative Work

One of the most interesting uses of this program is comparing how the same concept appears across languages and systems. For example, searching for the value of the Hebrew word for "light" (אור = 207 in Hechrachi) in a Greek text, using Isopsephy, would find Greek words or phrases that happen to total 207. Whether such equalities are meaningful is a matter of interpretation.

The Multi-Language Compare checkbox in the Gematria panel automates this: for each word found matching your target value in one language, it also shows what that same word totals in all other language systems — giving you a quick cross-reference view.

The Gematria Reference page (linked in the footer) shows the complete value table for every letter in every system. Use it to understand why a particular word has the value it does.

Chapter 6 — Gematria in the App

The Gematria Panel

The Gematria panel sits below the ELS search controls in the left column. It has three parts: a system selector, a live calculator, and a value search.

Live Calculator

Type any word or phrase into the Calculate value field. The result box appears immediately showing:

  • The total gematria value in large numerals
  • A per-letter breakdown showing each letter's individual value as a small chip
  • The digital root (shown only when it differs from the total)

Clicking the large number copies it into the Search by value field below, so you can immediately search the loaded text for other words with the same value.

The system selector at the top of the panel controls which gematria system is used for both the calculator and the search. When you change the primary language in the header, the system selector automatically switches to the most appropriate system for that language.

Searching by Value

Enter a numeric value in the Search by value field and click 🔢 Search. The program will scan all the text currently loaded (from the last ELS search) and find every word or letter sequence whose gematria in the selected system equals that value.

Note: A text must already be loaded before gematria search will work. Perform an ELS search first (even with an empty result set) to load the text into memory, then use gematria search on that text.

Word Mode vs. Sequence Mode

Two search modes are available, controlled by the radio buttons under Search mode:

Words
Searches the original verse text (with spaces and punctuation preserved) for individual words whose gematria value matches the target. Results show the word, its verse reference, and the verse text with the matching word highlighted in amber.
Letter sequences
Searches the continuous normalized text for any run of consecutive letters (minimum and maximum length configurable) whose cumulative gematria equals the target. This is a more exhaustive search — similar in spirit to ELS, but based on value rather than spelling. Results include a "View in Text" button that highlights the matching letters in the Text View.

Word mode is faster and more interpretively natural. Sequence mode can find patterns that cross word boundaries — useful for advanced research.

Multi-Language Compare

When the Compare across all languages checkbox is ticked, each result in the gematria results list gains a row of coloured chips showing what value the same word has in every other language system. For example, a Hebrew word matching your target value might show:

LATIN: 87 KJV: 112 FINNISH: 99 GREEK: 340

This lets you quickly check whether a match in one language has any cross-language significance — for instance, whether the same word also equals 26 (YHWH) in the English system.

Note that this comparison applies the default system for each language to the same word as typed. Since the alphabets are different, letters not in the target system's alphabet will have a value of 0 and be silently skipped. This is most meaningful when comparing words that share a common transliteration.

Chapter 7 — Kabbalah

What Is Kabbalah?

Kabbalah (קַבָּלָה — literally "receiving" or "tradition") is the mystical dimension of Judaism: a body of teachings that attempts to understand the hidden nature of God, the structure of creation, and humanity's place within it. It is the spiritual tradition from which both gematria and the idea of a hidden Bible Code directly descend.

Where mainstream rabbinic Judaism focuses on halakha (legal and ethical conduct) and midrash (narrative interpretation), Kabbalah adds a third layer: sod (secret or mystical meaning). Kabbalists hold that the Torah is not merely a historical or legal text, but a living divine document whose every word, letter, and even the spaces between letters encode cosmic truths about the nature of reality.

This is not metaphor for Kabbalists — it is a literal claim about language. The Hebrew alphabet, in Kabbalistic thought, is not merely a writing system invented by humans. Each of the 22 letters is a primordial force, a channel of divine energy through which the world was spoken into existence. God created the universe with language, and Hebrew letters are the building blocks of that creation. This theology makes every technique for finding hidden meaning in Hebrew letters — gematria, ELS, Notarikon, Temurah — not a game or a puzzle, but a form of contemplative prayer and theological inquiry.

Origins and Historical Development

Kabbalah has roots stretching back to the earliest strata of Jewish mysticism, though it crystallised as a distinct tradition in 12th–13th century southern France and Spain. The key historical milestones are:

The Sefirot and the Hebrew Letters

The central diagram of Kabbalah is the Etz Chayyim (Tree of Life): a network of ten divine attributes called sefirot (singular: sefirah) connected by 22 paths. The sefirot are not separate gods or beings — they are aspects or "vessels" of the one infinite God (called Ein Sof, "without end") through which divine energy flows into the created world.

The ten sefirot are: Keter (Crown), Chokhmah (Wisdom), Binah (Understanding), Chesed (Lovingkindness), Gevurah (Strength/Judgement), Tiferet (Beauty/Harmony), Netzach (Victory/Eternity), Hod (Splendour), Yesod (Foundation), and Malkhut (Kingdom). Their total gematria value in Hebrew is a subject of extensive Kabbalistic study.

Crucially, the 22 paths connecting the sefirot correspond to the 22 letters of the Hebrew alphabet. Each letter is therefore both a sound, a number, and a cosmic pathway. The Sefer Yetzirah divides the letters into three groups — three "mothers" (Alef, Mem, Shin), seven "doubles" (letters with two pronunciations), and twelve "simples" — each group governing a different aspect of space, time, and the human body.

This is why gematria is not felt by Kabbalists to be merely numerological play. Because each letter is a divine pathway, two words sharing the same numerical value are not merely numerically equivalent — they are connected through the same divine channel. The number reveals a structural identity at the level of cosmic architecture.

The Four Levels of Interpretation (PaRDeS)

Classical Jewish hermeneutics recognises four levels of biblical interpretation, whose initial letters form the word PaRDeS (פַּרְדֵּס — orchard, and the origin of the English word "paradise"):

Peshat (פְּשָׁט)
The plain, literal meaning of the text. What the words say on the surface. Standard biblical scholarship operates almost entirely at this level.
Remez (רֶמֶז)
"Hint" — allegorical or philosophical meaning encoded in the text. Gematria belongs primarily here: numbers hint at deeper structural connections between ideas. Medieval Jewish philosophers like Maimonides and Nachmanides worked extensively at this level.
Derash (דְּרַשׁ)
"Seek" — midrashic and homiletical interpretation; the creative, narrative exploration of what a text might teach beyond its surface. Rabbinic literature (Talmud, Midrash) operates largely here.
Sod (סוֹד)
"Secret" — the mystical and esoteric meaning, accessible only through Kabbalistic study. ELS and advanced gematria techniques belong here. This is the level at which Kabbalah operates, and it is understood to be the most profound and most dangerous layer of interpretation.

The tradition holds that the Sod level should only be approached by those already deeply grounded in the other three levels — hence the classical rule that Kabbalah study was reserved for married men over forty with a solid foundation in Talmud. Modern practice is considerably more open, but the principle of building up through the levels remains a core pedagogical ideal.

Notarikon and Temurah: Kabbalah's Other Letter Arts

Gematria is the best-known of Kabbalah's three primary letter-interpretation techniques, but it works alongside two others:

Notarikon (נוֹטָרִיקוֹן)
Treating letters as acronyms. A word's letters are expanded into a phrase (each letter becomes the first letter of a new word), or a phrase is compressed into an acronym-word. The classic example: the word Anochi (אָנֹכִי — "I") at the start of the Ten Commandments is read by some as an acronym for Ana Nafshi Ketavit Yehavit ("I Myself wrote and gave [the Torah]"). The name PaRDeS itself is a notarikon of the four interpretive levels.
Temurah (תְּמוּרָה)
Letter substitution according to fixed cipher rules. The most famous is Atbash (אתבש): the first letter of the alphabet (Alef) is swapped with the last (Tav), the second (Bet) with the second-to-last (Shin), and so on. Atbash appears in the Hebrew Bible itself: the prophet Jeremiah writes "Sheshach" (שֵׁשַׁךְ) — an Atbash encoding of "Babel" (בָּבֶל, Babylon) — apparently to disguise a politically dangerous reference. Another common substitution is Albam, which divides the alphabet in half and swaps the two halves. Kabbalistic literature lists dozens of such ciphers, some of recursive complexity.

These three techniques — gematria, notarikon, and temurah — are collectively called the Sod ha-Otiyot (Secret of the Letters) and form the core toolkit of Kabbalistic textual analysis. Medieval scholars compiled encyclopaedic tables of equivalences derived from all three methods, and Kabbalistic commentaries on scripture often deploy all three simultaneously in a single interpretive move.

Kabbalah and the Bible Code

The modern ELS Bible Code movement did not emerge from nowhere — it has clear Kabbalistic antecedents stretching back many centuries. The key figures in this lineage are:

  • Rabbi Bahya ben Asher (Spain, 1255–1340) — In his Torah commentary, he notes that the word TORAH (תורה) appears at 50-letter intervals starting from the beginning of each of the Five Books of Moses, and that its letters appear at 42-letter intervals in the opening chapter of Genesis. He attributed this to divine authorship and cited it as evidence that the Torah's structure itself is a form of revelation.
  • Rabbi Moses Cordovero (Safed, 1522–1570) — Compiled Pardes Rimonim (Garden of Pomegranates), an encyclopaedic Kabbalistic work that codified dozens of gematria methods and noted various ELS-like patterns in the Torah. He was the first to systematise these observations into a comprehensive methodology.
  • Rabbi Michael Ber Weissmandl (Slovakia, 1903–1957) — In the years before World War II, Weissmandl — a Torah scholar and mathematician — found extensive ELS patterns in the Torah using hand calculation (before computers existed). His manuscript, posthumously published as Torah Menuchasecha (1958), is the direct precursor to the computer-assisted ELS research of Witztum and Rips.
  • Eliyahu Rips (Riga/Jerusalem, 1948–2024) — A professor of mathematics at the Hebrew University of Jerusalem and an observant Jew, Rips was directly motivated by Kabbalistic theology in his ELS research. He saw the statistical patterns he found as confirmation of the Kabbalistic claim that the Torah's structure transcends human authorship.

The Kabbalistic tradition holds that the Torah given to Moses at Sinai was transmitted as a continuous stream of letters without spaces — Torah she-bikhtav. The spaces and word divisions we read today were a secondary interpretation. ELS research implicitly works with this idea: it removes all spaces and reads the letters as one continuous stream.

Western Kabbalah and Cross-Cultural Influence

From the 15th century onwards, Christian humanist scholars became fascinated by Kabbalah and began developing a parallel tradition of Christian and then Hermetic Kabbalah (often spelled "Qabalah" in Western contexts to distinguish it from Jewish tradition). This cross-pollination had enormous consequences for Western esotericism, magic, and even — indirectly — for modern gematria systems in Latin and English.

  • Giovanni Pico della Mirandola (Italy, 1463–1494) — The Italian Renaissance philosopher was among the first Christian scholars to study Kabbalah seriously. He argued in his Conclusions (1486) that Kabbalah provided proof of Christian doctrines, setting off a vogue for Christian Kabbalah among Renaissance humanists. The word "gematria" first appears in English through translations of his work.
  • Heinrich Cornelius Agrippa (Germany, 1486–1535) — His De Occulta Philosophia (1531) synthesised Kabbalah with Neoplatonism and ceremonial magic, and defined the first systematic gematria system for the Latin alphabet. This is the direct ancestor of the Latin ordinal gematria used in this program.
  • The Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn (UK, founded 1888) — The most influential Western esoteric order of the 19th century made Qabalah the centrepiece of its curriculum, associating the Hebrew letters with tarot cards, astrological signs, and the 22 paths of the Tree of Life. Members included W.B. Yeats and Aleister Crowley. This tradition is the origin of most modern English gematria systems.

The Finnish gematria system included in this program has no direct Kabbalistic heritage — it is a modern adaptation of the ordinal logic for linguistic study. However, the existence of gematria in the Finnish context reflects how broadly the idea of letter-number relationships has spread across cultures in the modern era.

Chapter 8 — Further Reading

Links & Resources

The following resources range from peer-reviewed academic papers to active research communities and comprehensive reference sites. They are listed not as endorsements of any particular conclusion, but as starting points for your own investigation.

Academic Papers

These are the core peer-reviewed publications that define the scientific debate around Bible codes. Read the originals, not just summaries.

Academic · 1994 Witztum, Rips & Rosenberg — "Equidistant Letter Sequences in the Book of Genesis" (1994)
Statistical Science, Vol. 9(3), pp. 429–438 · ResearchGate

The paper that started everything. Published in the peer-reviewed journal Statistical Science as a "challenging puzzle," it reported a p-value of 0.000016 for the clustering of rabbi names with their birth and death dates as ELS in the Book of Genesis. The journal's editor noted that referees "were baffled" by the result. This is essential primary reading — form your own view of the methodology before accepting any secondary account.

Critical · 1999 McKay, Bar-Natan, Bar-Hillel & Kalai — "Solving the Bible Code Puzzle" (1999)
Statistical Science, Vol. 14(2), pp. 150–173 · Project Euclid

The definitive scientific refutation, published in the same journal five years later and reviewed by four independent professional statisticians. The paper argues that the WRR result was "fatally defective" — the extraordinary sensitivity of ELS statistics to small changes in word spellings, combined with substantial flexibility in how the researchers chose their data, is sufficient to explain the result without invoking hidden codes. The paper demonstrated equivalent "codes" in a Hebrew translation of War and Peace. The journal editor wrote in the introduction that "the puzzle has been solved."

Critical · Ongoing Brendan McKay's Bible Codes Research Archive
users.cecs.anu.edu.au/~bdm/dilugim/torah.html · Australian National University

Professor McKay's comprehensive archive of the ELS debate, hosted at the Australian National University. Includes the full text of multiple papers, point-by-point rebuttals of Bible Code claims, demonstrations of equivalent "codes" found in Moby Dick and War and Peace, and a detailed account of the Hebrew spelling flexibility that underlies the controversy. Even if you believe in Bible codes, understanding the arguments here is essential for any honest assessment of the evidence.

Academic · 2019 Wilson — "Bible Code, Revisited" (2019)
Perspectives on Science and Christian Faith, Vol. 71(1) · Semantic Scholar

A more recent academic re-examination of the ELS controversy, attempting to assess where the debate stands a quarter-century after the original papers. Useful for understanding how the field has evolved and what (if anything) remains genuinely open. Approaches the question from a perspective sympathetic to theistic inquiry while maintaining academic rigour.

Critical Analysis & Balanced Commentary

These resources approach the Bible Code with scepticism but provide fair-minded analysis that is valuable reading regardless of your position.

Critical · Wikipedia Wikipedia: Bible Code
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bible_code

A thorough and well-sourced overview of the entire ELS controversy, covering the WRR paper, the MBBK rebuttal, subsequent experiments, and the positions of notable figures including Nobel laureate Robert Aumann, who called the evidence "suggestive but not conclusive." The article covers both proponent and critic arguments fairly and includes extensive references to primary sources.

Essay · First Things "The Bible by Numbers" — First Things
firstthings.com/the-bible-by-numbers/

A thoughtful philosophical essay engaging with both the statistical claims and the theological implications of Bible Code research. Draws interesting parallels between the statistical methodology used in ELS research and arguments from biological design. Takes the question seriously without being credulous. Useful for thinking through the philosophical stakes of the debate, not just the statistical ones.
